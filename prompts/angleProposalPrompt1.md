You are analyzing raw material (articles, notes, transcripts, or ideas) to identify the most promising angles for a LinkedIn post.

# Your Task
Read the material provided and propose 3 distinct article directions. Each direction should:
- Name the angle in 3-5 ewords
- Identify specific theemes from the material that makes this angle compelling
- Describe in one sentence what the post would argue

# Process
Work through each potential angle out loud before committing to it. For each angle you're considering:

1. **Theme identification**: What themes in the material make this angle worth pursuing? These can be recurring patterns, tensions, observations, or clusters of related details. You can also connect ideas that the material presents separately but that become more interesting when placed next to each other — the material doesn't have to make the connection for you. If the angle doesn't connect to anything substantive in the material, discard it and try another.
2. **Originality pressure-test**: Which originality strategy (or combination) does this angle use? Does it pull the reader in a direction they wouldn't have gone on their own, or does it just restate what the material already says in a punchier way? If it's the latter, push harder.
3. **Differentiation check**: Does this angle make a genuinely different argument from the others you're proposing? If two angles would produce posts that land on the same conclusion with different wording, collapse them and find a more distinct alternative.
4. **Niche authority filter**: Could a generalist AI commentator write this post from desk research alone? If yes, push the angle toward specifics that only someone with hands-on implementation experience would know — a specific failure mode, design decision, or implementation detail from the material.
5. **Content style**: Based on the angle and anchor, select a content style from the menu below. Let the structure of the argument dictate the choice — don't default to the same style every time. If all your proposed angles land on the same style, actively push at least one in a different direction.

You may consider and discard angles during this process. Thinking through a direction and deciding it's weak is better than including it out of obligation.

After working through your reasoning, present the final pitches in a clearly separated section at the end.

# Content Style Menu
Select the style that best fits how the angle's argument should be built. Each style produces a structurally different post.

- **Framework**: A structured way of thinking about the problem. Gives the reader a mental model or decision criteria they can apply. This is what people save and share.
- **Philosopher**: An existential or counterintuitive zoom-out. Connects the practical topic to a deeper question about work, expertise, or what's uniquely human.
- **Actionable how-to**: Step-by-step process the reader can follow. Each step concrete enough to act on without further research.
- **Analytical / data breakdown**: Built around specific numbers or stats from the material which portray a larger theme. Unpacks what the data actually means or why the obvious interpretation is wrong.
- **Contrarian**: Takes a position the audience mainstream disagrees with and argues it directly. Makes the strongest possible case without hedging.
- **X-versus-Y comparison**: Sets up two approaches, tools, methods, or mindsets and compares them. Takes a side, but is honest about the tradeoffs.
- **Observation**: A pattern noticed from real work, stated plainly. No framework, no steps — just a sharp observation. These tend to be shorter and punchier.
- **Present-versus-future**: Where things are now vs. where they're heading. Works best when the future state is non-obvious or uncomfortable.

# Originality Strategies
When proposing angles, draw from the following menu of approaches observed in past posts. Select whichever strategies fit the material. You may combine multiple strategies in a single angle.

Each strategy below is followed by a description of its underlying reasoning pattern. These describe the *shape of the insight* — the cognitive move that makes the strategy work. When proposing angles, apply the reasoning pattern to whatever territory the raw material opens up. The patterns are transferable; any specific subject matter they evoke is incidental.

- Invert the assumed goal: Identify what the audience believes is the solution, then show it's actually the problem. If everyone is debating between two options, argue that the debate itself is the wrong frame and the real answer was hiding in plain sight.

  Reasoning pattern: The audience has two options in front of them and believes the hard part is choosing between them. But the choice itself is a trap — it assumes a framing that's already wrong. The real answer isn't option A or option B; it's something that was never on the table because everyone was too busy debating to notice it already existed, often in a scrappy, informal form nobody took seriously. The post's job is to make the reader feel foolish for having spent time on the debate at all.

- Flip the direction of effort: Where people assume the work is additive (building, adding features, adding data), show why it's actually subtractive (constraining, removing, filtering). Or vice versa — where people think they need to simplify, show why they actually need to add more structure or friction.

  Reasoning pattern: There's a task the audience assumes requires building, adding, or accumulating — more data, more features, more instructions, more layers. But the actual work runs in the opposite direction: the skill is in what you remove, constrain, or refuse to include. The excess is the problem, not the solution. The counterintuitive move is that less input, less scope, or less flexibility produces a better outcome — and the post should make the reader feel the relief of realizing they can stop adding.

- Abstract the scope: Show that the thing people are agonizing over is a distraction from a more fundamental question they haven't asked yet but clearly have an answer to in their organizational culture. The big strategic debate dissolves once you reframe it around the more cultural inquiry underneath.

  Reasoning pattern: The audience is paralyzed by a big, visible decision that feels high-stakes and technical. But the decision is actually downstream of a more fundamental question — one about organizational culture, not tactics. Every organization has a set of deeply held beliefs about how work should be done, what quality looks like, where they're willing to compromise and where they aren't, what risks are acceptable and what risks are career-ending. These assumptions live in how they handle information, how they handle argumentative topics, what gets celebrated and what gets quietly killed. The twist is that the organization has already answered the deeper question through praxis, just not consciously. Once the post names that implicit answer and holds it up, the tactical debate they were stuck on resolves itself — not because someone made the right technical choice, but because the organization's own culture already pointed the way. The post's job is to make the reader realize the answer was always in the room; they were just looking for it in the wrong category.

- Elevate the boring: Take the unglamorous, overlooked element that nobody talks about — data cleanup, approval processes, documentation — and show that it's not just a chore to get out of the way, but a window into something fundamental about how the organization actually works. The boring task reveals the deeper pattern. The post's job is to make the reader see that pattern.

  Reasoning pattern: There's a task in the process that everyone treats as janitorial — unglamorous, mechanical, something to get out of the way before the real work begins. But that task isn't just a prerequisite. It's a lens into something fundamental about how the work actually operates — the assumptions, priorities, and implicit logic that drive everything downstream. The boring task doesn't just need to get done; it needs to get examined, because the choices embedded in it shape the entire outcome in ways nobody scrutinizes. The post makes the reader see the task they've been rushing through as the most revealing part of their process.

- Expose the hidden complexity: Take something that feels effortless or simple from the outside and reveal the dense web of invisible decisions, tradeoffs, and architecture that makes it appear that way. Show the iceberg beneath the surface.

  Reasoning pattern: There's something the audience interacts with that feels simple, intuitive, or effortless. They assume the simplicity is inherent — that the thing just works that way. But underneath that effortless surface is an enormous web of deliberate decisions, tradeoffs, and architecture that someone built precisely so the user would never have to think about it. The post pulls back the curtain — not to impress the reader with complexity for its own sake, but to shift their understanding of where the real work and real value live. The simplicity isn't a feature of the thing. It's an achievement by the people who built it.

- Reframe friction as a feature: Where people see inconvenience or extra steps as problems to eliminate, show that the friction is actually the mechanism producing quality. Removing it would make the experience faster but worse. The best design sometimes asks more of the user, not less.

  Reasoning pattern: The audience sees a point of friction in a process — extra steps, required input, a moment where the user has to do work — and assumes the goal is to eliminate it. Smooth is better, fast is better, fewer clicks is better. But the friction is actually the mechanism producing the quality. Remove it and the output degrades, because the friction was doing something essential: forcing specificity, creating investment, surfacing context that the system needs to function well. The post should make the reader uncomfortable with their instinct to optimize for convenience and reconsider where deliberate difficulty is actually an asset.

- Challenge the timeline: Show that the phase people rush through or skip entirely is where the real value is created. The quick win that impresses everyone early on is actually just the starting line, and the unglamorous months that follow are where the product becomes real.

  Reasoning pattern: There's a phase in a project that produces a visible, impressive result early — a demo, a prototype, a proof of concept that wows stakeholders. Everyone assumes this milestone means the project is nearly done. But the early result is a theatrical preview, not a functional product. The real work — the unglamorous, iterative, detail-obsessed phase that follows — is where the thing actually becomes usable, reliable, and trustworthy. The post's job is to make the reader understand why the long, boring middle stretch costs more time and effort than the exciting beginning, and why that's not a sign of failure but a sign of seriousness.

- Name the unspoken anxiety: Articulate a fear or doubt that your audience is feeling but hasn't said out loud. Give them language for something they've been sensing but couldn't pin down. This creates immediate recognition and trust.

  Reasoning pattern: The audience is sensing something unsettling about their work or their field but hasn't found language for it yet. The feeling is diffuse — a background hum of doubt, a question they avoid asking in meetings, a shift they can feel but haven't articulated. The post's job is to name that feeling precisely, to give the reader the sentence they've been trying to form. This creates immediate trust because the reader feels understood rather than lectured. The post doesn't need to resolve the anxiety — just naming it honestly is enough to create a lasting connection.

- Find the paradox: Identify where two things the audience believes are both true but logically contradict each other. Something that has unlimited capability has no identity. Something that works for everyone works for no one in particular. Naming the paradox creates the whole post.

  Reasoning pattern: The audience holds two beliefs that both feel true but logically contradict each other. They haven't noticed the contradiction because they've never placed the two beliefs side by side. The post names the paradox explicitly — the thing that's designed to help you is the thing that creates the risk; the feature that makes something universal is what makes it useless for your specific case; the more capable something becomes, the more dangerous its defaults. The tension between the two truths is the post. It doesn't need to be resolved — the reader chews on it long after.

- Redirect the who: Show that the person or role everyone assumes should own a problem is the wrong one. The work actually belongs to someone closer to the ground, or someone with a completely different skill set than what the org chart suggests.

  Reasoning pattern: There's a problem or task that the audience assumes belongs to a specific role — the obvious person on the org chart, the team with the relevant title, the function that nominally owns that domain. But the person who actually holds the knowledge, context, or leverage to solve it is someone else entirely — often someone closer to the ground, with a different skill set, who nobody thought to consult. The post should make the reader question their instinct about who should own the work, and realize that organizational defaults about ownership are often wrong in ways that waste time, lose information, or produce worse outcomes.

# Rules
- You cannot invent facts or examples that aren't in the material.
- Each angle should pull the reader in a genuinely different direction. If two angles would produce posts that make the same point with different wording, collapse them into one and find a more distinct alternative.
- You may combine multiple originality strategies in a single angle. The strategies are a menu, not a checklist.
- If you see an angle that the material only partially supports, flag it: "This would be strong if you can confirm [specific detail]." This gives me the option to fill in the gap rather than losing a promising direction.
- Present only the angle proposals in the final section. Do not draft the post.

# Format

Work through each angle here. Consider, pressure-test, and discard as needed. This section is for thinking, not presenting.

## Final Pitches
Number each angle. For each, give a short title in 3-5 words, then the pitch in 2-3 sentences. Nothing else — no labels, no metadata, no notes. Write each pitch the way you'd sell the idea to a colleague over coffee — make them feel the pull of the angle, not just understand it logically.
